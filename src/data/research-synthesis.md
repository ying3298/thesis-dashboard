# Take Your Time — Research-to-Design Synthesis

**Purpose:** Evidence chain for MFA thesis presentation and committee review.
**Data basis:** 4 semi-structured interviews (P1–P4), affinity mapping, 12 design constraints, 11 hypotheses, synthesis analysis.
**Honesty note:** n=4. Every claim below is tagged with how many participants support it. Where the chain is held together by design philosophy rather than participant data, it is flagged explicitly.

---

## 1. Problem Statement (Settled)

> When families live together, connection happens without trying. You hear your parent cooking. You notice they look tired. No one decides to connect — shared space does the work. This ambient presence carries the relationship, especially in families where love is shown through action rather than words.
>
> When the child moves abroad, this ambient layer disappears completely. What remains is intentional communication only — and the tools designed to support it create two problems.

**The two-failure framing:**
- **Too active** (calls, video, texts) → demands emotional labor, unsustainable
- **Too passive** (friendship lamps, presence indicators) → shows presence but doesn't sustain engagement, no feedback, no reason to keep showing up

**The design opportunity lives in the narrow space between these two failures.**

### Cross-Referencing the Problem Statement Against Participant Data

Each load-bearing claim in the problem statement is tested below against what participants actually said.

| Claim | P1 | P2 | P3 | P4 | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ambient presence carried connection before separation** | "Changes only show up when you go home" — messages compress reality | "Two different time axes, Taipei's seems to move faster" | Implicit: 報備 system is her replacement for lost ambient layer | Childhood "dates" with mom; grandma's warmth "doesn't survive the phone" | 4/4 — Strong |
| **Synchronous tools create emotional labor** | "If I'm already suffering, I don't want to also filter the message for them" | "Being the lubricant and trash can for family... Family stuff I carry inside my body" | "I won't talk about things where you can't do anything to help" — filters by practicality | "Thinking of her doesn't really trigger me to do anything" — the Pad Thai Rule | 4/4 — Strong |
| **Ambient-only tools fail to sustain engagement** | No direct statement | No direct statement | "Sticker = mission accomplished" — loop closes, nothing builds | Predicted he'd stop drawing on shared lock screen: "he'd eventually stop writing anything" | 1/4 direct. See flag below. |
| **Parent-child separation differs from romantic distance** | "When they annoy me, I can truly disappear" — midway adult | "Being the lubricant and trash can for family is different from being that for friends" | Implicit: 報備 is a parent-specific duty | "I quite like this distance — I have a lot of space for my own development" | 2/4 explicit, 2/4 implicit |

> **FLAG 1: The "too passive" failure.** The claim that ambient-only tools fail to sustain engagement is the *weaker half* of the two-failure framing. Participants describe the problems with active tools extensively (4/4). But they don't really use ambient tools, so they can't report on their failure. P4's prediction about disengaging from a shared lock screen is the closest direct evidence. The "too passive" critique draws more from the designer's analysis of existing products (friendship lamps, presence indicators) than from participant testimony. This is an honest design critique, but the committee should know the asymmetry: the "too active" failure is participant-confirmed; the "too passive" failure is designer-diagnosed.

## 2. Findings Clusters

Five clusters emerged when participant data was organized against the problem statement. Clusters are ranked by the number of participants who contributed evidence.

### Cluster A: The Effort Barrier (4/4)
*Every connection requires crossing a threshold. All four participants need a "reason" to reach out.*

| Participant | Evidence |
|---|---|
| P1 | "When something comes up, you just press a 'thinking of you' button." Content (sprouted food, cat photos) is an excuse for contact. |
| P2 | Last call to dad was to ask about a physical structure for school. Practical question = permission slip. |
| P3 | 報備 system: "Let them know what I'm doing, I'm not dead." The entire communication architecture is reporting, not expressing. |
| P4 | The Pad Thai Rule: only calls grandma with a concrete reason. "Thinking of her doesn't really trigger me to do anything." |

**Synthesis insight:** The universal practical excuse pattern is the single most robust finding across all four participants. It is confirmed independently in each interview without prompting. This is the bedrock.

### Cluster B: The Emotional Cost of Transparency (4/4)
*Sharing is managed, not free. Every participant curates what the other side sees.*

| Participant | Evidence |
|---|---|
| P1 | "I want it to stay flat. I don't want them to know how happy or sad I am." If they see happy days, they'll notice when happy days stop. |
| P2 | She IS the family's emotional infrastructure — "lubricant" and "trash can." She carries family stuff "inside my body." |
| P3 | Filters by practicality: "I won't talk about things where you can't do anything to help." She values being "the narrator of her own story." |
| P4 | Wants "decorative and non-functional" — explicitly anti-surveillance. "Not functional." |

**Key tension within this cluster:** P1 and P4 want opacity (hide emotion). P2 wants transparency (read emotion). P3 wants control (choose what to share). This is a genuine, unresolved tension — not a flaw to fix but a condition to design around. The synthesis data maps this: Wants Transparency ↔ Wants Opacity spectrum shows P2 at 20 (wants transparency) vs. P1 at 75, P4 at 80 (want opacity).

### Cluster C: Invisible Change / Compressed Time (4/4)
*Distance distorts how families perceive each other. Changes accumulate invisibly until a visit makes them suddenly real.*

| Participant | Evidence |
|---|---|
| P1 | "Their expression is very one-sided. When you go home, you find out it's not like that at all." New household rules she never heard about. |
| P2 | "We're like on two different time axes, and Taipei's seems to move faster." Half a year abroad felt short; half a year in Taipei felt long. |
| P3 | "Time really is passing" (真的是時間有在過耶) — first thing she notices about mom is signs of aging. But she would never say it. |
| P4 | Grandma's warmth doesn't survive the phone. In person: warm, cooks. On phone: brief, cold, rushing to hang up. |

**Connected universal insight:** All four read parents through sensory/physical features, not words. P1: hair. P2: under-eye area. P3: overall appearance/aging. P4: eyes. This bypasses verbal communication entirely.

### Cluster D: Low-Content Connection Works (4/4)
*The signal doesn't need to carry information. Presence matters more than content.*

| Participant | Evidence |
|---|---|
| P1 | Cat photo loop is the ONLY reliable daily interaction. "It's like I publish a quest, and my parents have to complete it." Zero emotional content. |
| P2 | "My family's relationship is already like magic." She doesn't want to change the communication — just wants to feel presence. |
| P3 | "Sticker = mission accomplished" (達成了這樣). Getting a sticker back is fully sufficient. The loop closes with minimal effort. |
| P4 | Wants something "decorative, ambient, zero-function" (裝飾性的東西... 不是功能性的). |

**What this cluster establishes:** A daily gesture that carries no content but confirms "I'm here, you're here" would serve all four archetypes — P1's privacy-seeking minimalism, P2's emotional reading, P3's efficient reporting, P4's ambient observing.

### Cluster E: Retrospective Value (2/4 direct, 4/4 inferred)
*Looking back matters more than real-time feedback.*

| Participant | Evidence |
|---|---|
| P1 | "Sad things are always specific, but happy things are sometimes blurry." She journals when low but treasures happy entries most. "It was always going to change" — each day on a calendar is already different. |
| P2 | Evening longing is existential: "There's no umbrella here that you know will always hold you." Morning longing is different — positive, wishing parents could see a view. |
| P3 | (No direct statement about retrospection) |
| P4 | (No direct statement about retrospection) |

> **FLAG 2: Retrospective accumulation.** Direct evidence for wanting to "look back" comes from P1 (journaling, calendar insight) and P2 (temporal longing). P3 and P4 did not articulate retrospective value. Design constraint #4 ("Retrospective accumulation — neither wants real-time feedback, but both value looking back") is derived from P1 and P2 only. It was extended to a design principle based on the designer's judgment that accumulation is what separates the proposed design from too-passive ambient tools. This is a reasonable inference but not a 4/4 universal.

## 3. How Might We — Three Framings, Ranked by Evidence

The handoff document proposed three HMW candidates. Below, each is scored against the clusters.

### HMW 1 (Ranked #1 — Strongest Evidence)

> **How might we create a form of connection where input feels low-effort but accumulates into something both people value over time?**

| Evidence source | Cluster | Support |
|---|---|---|
| Universal practical excuse pattern | Cluster A | 4/4 — "low-effort" is demanded by all four communication styles |
| Low-content connection works | Cluster D | 4/4 — content isn't needed; presence is |
| Retrospective accumulation | Cluster E | 2/4 direct (P1, P2), 2/4 inferred |
| Two-failure framing | Problem Statement | Directly addresses BOTH failures: low-effort solves the too-active problem; accumulation solves the too-passive engagement decay problem |

**Why this is the strongest:** It is the only HMW that addresses both sides of the two-failure framing in a single statement. "Low-effort" tackles the emotional labor problem. "Accumulates into something both people value" tackles the engagement decay problem. Every participant contributed evidence to at least one half.

### HMW 2 (Ranked #2 — Moderate Evidence)

> **How might we rebuild ambient presence for families separated by time zones — without creating new emotional labor?**

| Evidence source | Cluster | Support |
|---|---|---|
| Emotional cost of transparency | Cluster B | 4/4 |
| Effort barrier | Cluster A | 4/4 |
| Addresses the too-active failure | Problem Statement | Strong |
| Addresses the too-passive failure | Problem Statement | **Weak** — "ambient presence" risks collapsing back into friendship-lamp territory |

**Limitation:** This HMW addresses one failure well (too-active → emotional labor) but is vague about how it avoids the other failure (too-passive → no sustained engagement). "Rebuild ambient presence" could describe a friendship lamp. The word "accumulates" is what separates HMW 1 from HMW 2.

### HMW 3 (Ranked #3 — Weakest Evidence)

> **How might we design a shared daily ritual that uses the time zone gap as a processing window rather than a barrier?**

| Evidence source | Cluster | Support |
|---|---|---|
| P2's evening longing | Cluster E | 1/4 direct |
| P1's time zone messaging | Cluster A | 1/4 (mentions time zone as reason for async) |
| "Processing window" concept | — | 0/4 — no participant used this framing |

> **FLAG 3: "Gap as processing window" is a designer reframe, not a participant insight.** Participants describe the time zone gap as a *constraint* or *obstacle* ("I'm not sure if they'll pick up" — P1). The reframe — that the gap can be a *processing window*, time to reflect and respond with care — is the designer's interpretive move. It is a compelling design philosophy. But no participant said "I wish the gap were useful" or described using delay productively. P2's evening longing supports the temporal dimension, but she experiences it as pain ("There's no umbrella here"), not as a processing opportunity. This HMW is elegant but data-thin. It should be presented as a design hypothesis, not a research finding.

## 4. Chosen HMW → Opportunity → Hypotheses

### Chosen HMW

> **How might we create a form of connection where input feels low-effort but accumulates into something both people value over time?**

**Reasoning:** Strongest evidence base (Clusters A + D = 4/4). Only framing that addresses both failures simultaneously. Accommodates all four communication archetypes without requiring resolution of the opacity-vs-transparency tension.

### Opportunity Statement

Families separated by time zones need a way to maintain connection that costs less than a message but builds more than a status light. The opportunity: design a shared object that turns tiny, repeatable gestures into visible proof that both people are still showing up — without requiring either person to decide what to say, when to say it, or how much to reveal.

### Hypothesis A (Evidence: Strong)

> If we provide a low-effort, ambient, daily ritual that doesn't require synchronous availability, it will reduce the emotional labor of staying connected and rebuild peripheral presence.

**Data support:** All 4 participants describe the effort barrier (Cluster A). All 4 describe wanting connection without composition (Cluster D). The practical excuse pattern (4/4) confirms that lowering the effort barrier is the critical variable — P1's "thinking of you button," P4's Pad Thai Rule, P3's sticker-as-completion, P2's practical question as permission slip.

### Hypothesis B (Evidence: Moderate)

> If the interaction accumulates into a shared artifact over time — something both people can see growing — it will create a sense of shared journey that makes the distance feel productive rather than empty.

**Data support:** P1's "It was always going to change" supports accumulation as inherently meaningful. P1's journaling habit supports retrospective value. Design constraint #4 (retrospective accumulation) is derived from P1 and P2.

> **FLAG 4: "Productive distance" is aspirational language.** No participant described distance as productive. P4 said he *likes* the distance ("a lot of space for my own development"), but that's appreciating separation, not finding it productive. P2 experiences distance as loss. "Makes the distance feel productive rather than empty" is a design aspiration — what the designer hopes the artifact will achieve — not something participants articulated. Honest framing: this hypothesis predicts an outcome that has not yet been observed.

## 5. Design Principles

Three principles, derived from the chosen HMW, ranked by evidence strength. Each is paired with the strongest participant quote(s).

### Principle 1: "Connection should cost less than a decision."
*If the system provides the reason, the emotional labor drops below the threshold of avoidance.*

**Derived from:** Cluster A (The Effort Barrier) — 4/4 participants.

| Participant | Quote |
|---|---|
| P1 | "When something comes up, you just press a 'thinking of you' button." |
| P4 | "Thinking of her doesn't really trigger me to do anything." |
| P3 | "達成了這樣" (Mission accomplished) — a sticker closes the loop. |
| P2 | Last call to dad was to ask about a physical structure for school — practical excuse as permission slip. |

**Evidence level: Strongest.** This principle sits on the most robust universal pattern in the data.

### Principle 2: "Small gestures, seen over time."
*Each small input should feel worthwhile because it contributes to something both people can see growing.*

**Derived from:** The two-failure framing (ambient tools fail because no feedback/accumulation) + Cluster E (retrospective value).

| Participant | Quote |
|---|---|
| P1 | "It was always going to change." — The calendar is already different each day. |
| P1 | "Happy things are sometimes blurry." — Retrospective artifacts preserve what memory loses. |
| P2 | "My family's relationship is already like magic." — Don't add, extend. |
| P3 | "好啊我們都還在過生活" (Good, we're all still living our lives.) — The baseline signal. |

**Evidence level: Moderate.** The "small gestures" half is strong (4/4 want minimal effort). The "seen over time" half draws from P1 primarily. The principle's real work is solving the too-passive failure — preventing engagement decay — which is more a design argument than a participant demand (see Flag 2).

### Principle 3: "Make the gap the material."
*The time zone difference is not a barrier to design around but a structural feature to design with.*

**Derived from:** The 13-hour gap as temporal asymmetry + P2's evening longing + the asynchronous turn-taking inherent in the proposed design.

| Participant | Quote |
|---|---|
| P2 | "There's no umbrella here that you know will always hold you." — Evening longing confirms the gap is emotionally loaded. |
| P1 | "Because of the time difference, I'm not sure if they'll pick up." — The gap shapes all behavior. |
| P2 | "We're like on two different time axes, and Taipei's seems to move faster." — Two timelines, one family. |
| P4 | "I quite like this distance — I have a lot of space for my own development." — Gap as positive. |

> **FLAG 5: This is the most philosophically driven principle.** Participants describe the gap as a constraint (P1), a source of pain (P2), or something they appreciate (P4). None described it as *material* or a *processing window*. The reframe — gap-as-material rather than gap-as-obstacle — is the designer's central intellectual contribution. It is what makes this thesis distinctive. But honest classification: it is a design hypothesis validated by absence of contradiction, not by presence of affirmation. The data says "the gap shapes everything." The principle says "therefore, design with it." The leap from observation to prescription is the designer's, and the committee should evaluate it as such.

### How the Three Principles Reinforce Each Other

They form a causal chain, not a list:

1. **"Make the gap the material"** establishes the temporal structure — one person wakes while the other sleeps, creating natural turn-taking.
2. **"Connection should cost less than a decision"** sets the effort threshold — within that temporal structure, each turn must be so effortless it doesn't feel like a decision.
3. **"Small gestures, seen over time"** provides the return on investment — if each effortless turn contributes to something visible, the cycle sustains itself rather than decaying.

Remove any one and the system breaks:
- Without Principle 3 (gap as material) → you lose the temporal structure that enables asynchronous turn-taking.
- Without Principle 1 (cost less than a decision) → you get the too-active failure (emotional labor).
- Without Principle 2 (seen over time) → you get the too-passive failure (engagement decay).

## 6. Three Concept Cards — Mapped to Principles

### Concept 2: Paired Calendar
**Evidence level: STRONGEST**

**What it is:** A shared physical tear-off calendar where family members contribute when the other is asleep. The 13-hour gap becomes a built-in turn-taking rhythm. Daily rip ritual as ambient presence signal. Physical calendars connected via WiFi. 30 days of rips accumulate into a shared artifact.

**Principles served:**
- **"Connection should cost less than a decision"** — Primary. The rip is a 2-second gesture. No content creation, no composition, no choosing what to say.
- **"Small gestures, seen over time"** — Primary. 30 days of torn pages tell a story one rip never could.
- **"Make the gap the material"** — Secondary. The calendar's asymmetric timing (one rips in the morning, the other in the evening) uses the gap as turn-taking structure.

**Participant quotes as proof:**

| Quote | Participant | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| "When something comes up, you just press a 'thinking of you' button." | P1 | The rip IS the thinking-of-you button — zero content, pure gesture. |
| "It was always going to change." | P1 | The calendar's daily change is inherently meaningful — no added mechanics needed. |
| "好啊我們都還在過生活" (Good, we're all still living.) | P3 | The rip confirms the baseline: we're both still here. That's enough. |
| "裝飾性的東西... 不是功能性的" (Decorative... not functional.) | P4 | Physical calendar as ambient object — decor, not technology. |
| "It's like I publish a quest, and my parents have to complete it." | P1 | Turn-taking ritual already exists in P1's cat photo loop. The calendar formalizes it. |
| "My family's relationship is already like magic." | P2 | The calendar extends what exists — invisible infrastructure, not a new product. |

**Design tensions it resolves:**
- P1 wants to hide emotion / P2 wants to read it → The rip's ambiguity serves both. P1 hides behind the neutral gesture; P2 reads meaning into timing.
- P3 wants efficiency / P2 carries emotional labor → Same rip, different meaning. P3: reporting. P2: presence.
- P4 wants decorative / P2 wants functional → Physical object (P4) carries digital trace (P2).

**Honest assessment:** This concept has the strongest connection to participant data and the most developed mechanism. It directly embodies Principles 1 and 2 with clear evidence. Its connection to Principle 3 (gap as material) is logical but inherits the philosophical character of that principle (Flag 5). Not yet tested with a real family pair.

### Concept 1: Clock Experiments (Time Dream)
**Evidence level: CONCEPTUAL**

**What it is:** Exploring how to make the dual-timezone experience visible and felt. Includes the "Time Dream" prototype (iOS lock screen, four time display modes). Most experimental, least defined.

**Principles served:**
- **"Make the gap the material"** — Primary. This concept is essentially a direct manifestation of Principle 3.
- **"Connection should cost less than a decision"** — Secondary. A lock screen glance costs zero effort.
- **"Small gestures, seen over time"** — Weak connection. No accumulation mechanism described.

**Participant quotes as proof:**

| Quote | Participant | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| "We're like on two different time axes, and Taipei's seems to move faster." | P2 | The dual-timeline experience is real and emotionally loaded. Making it visible addresses a felt need. |
| "Because of the time difference, I'm not sure if they'll pick up." | P1 | The time gap governs all connection behavior. Visualizing it makes the invisible visible. |
| "I quite like this distance — I have a lot of space for my own development." | P4 | For some, seeing the gap could reinforce a positive relationship with distance. |

> **FLAG 6: Concept 1's evidence chain has two weak links.** The concept is most strongly connected to Principle 3 ("Make the gap the material"), which is itself the least data-grounded principle (Flag 5). A concept built primarily on a philosophical principle is doubly speculative. Additionally, no participant asked to *see* the time gap differently — they described *experiencing* it. The leap from "participants experience temporal asymmetry" to "therefore, visualize it" is a design decision, not a research finding. The concept needs participant testing to validate whether making the gap visible produces connection or just information.

**Honest assessment:** This is the most experimental concept. It is a pure embodiment of the thesis's philosophical position (gap as material), which makes it intellectually interesting but empirically unproven. It lacks an accumulation mechanism, which means it risks the too-passive failure the problem statement identifies. Needs articulation of how it sustains engagement beyond novelty.

### Concept 3: AI Composed Dialogue
**Evidence level: LEAST DEFINED**

**What it is:** Not yet articulated in the handoff document. The concept involves AI mediation of family communication. Needs articulation of what it is, how it connects to the principles, and what research supports it.

**Principles served:**
- **"Connection should cost less than a decision"** — Potential. If AI reduces the composition burden, it lowers the effort barrier.
- **"Small gestures, seen over time"** — Unknown. No accumulation mechanism described.
- **"Make the gap the material"** — Weak. No clear temporal design.

**Participant quotes as proof (inferential, not direct):**

| Quote | Participant | What it could address |
|---|---|---|
| "My mom's walls — I can't see them. That's what scares me." | P2 | P2 wants emotional transparency that her mom won't provide. Could an AI surface emotional texture from neutral signals? |
| "Sad things are always specific, but happy things are sometimes blurry." | P1 | AI could help preserve and surface happy moments that memory loses. |
| "I can control this" (我可以掌控這件事情). | P3 | P3 values narrative control — AI mediation could either support this (curate what gets shared) or threaten it (surveillance by another name). |

> **FLAG 7: Concept 3's data connection is speculative.** No participant asked for AI mediation. No participant described a problem that explicitly calls for AI as the solution. The link is *inferred* from the gap between what P2 wants to know (emotional state) and what P1/P3/P4 are willing to share (minimal signal). An AI intermediary could theoretically navigate this opacity-transparency tension, but this is a design hypothesis with zero participant validation.
>
> **Additional risk:** The anti-surveillance instinct is universal (4/4). Any AI that interprets, translates, or composes on behalf of family members must contend with the fact that all four participants resist systems that feel like monitoring. P3's "I can control this" is especially relevant — AI mediation could feel like a loss of narrative control, which is the opposite of what she needs.

**Honest assessment:** This concept is the least defined, least evidence-backed, and most at risk of contradicting a universal participant need (anti-surveillance). It should be presented as an early-stage hypothesis, not as an equally developed member of the ecosystem. The committee should understand this is where the project is reaching beyond its current evidence.

## 8. All Flags — Where Philosophy Holds the Chain

1. **Flag 1 — The "too passive" half of the two-failure framing** has weaker participant evidence than the "too active" half. Participants confirmed emotional labor extensively. The critique of ambient tools is more the designer's analysis of existing products than participant testimony.

2. **Flag 2 — Retrospective accumulation** is directly evidenced by 2/4 participants (P1, P2). Extending it to a universal design requirement is a design judgment, not a research finding.

3. **Flag 3 — "Gap as processing window"** is a designer reframe. Participants experience the gap as constraint (P1), pain (P2), or positive space (P4). None described it as a processing opportunity.

4. **Flag 4 — "Productive distance"** is aspirational language. No participant described distance as productive. The hypothesis predicts an outcome not yet observed.

5. **Flag 5 — Principle 3 ("Make the gap the material")** is the most philosophically driven principle. It is the designer's central intellectual contribution and what makes the thesis distinctive, but it is validated by absence of contradiction rather than presence of affirmation.

6. **Flag 6 — Concept 1 (Clock Experiments)** is built primarily on the weakest principle and lacks an accumulation mechanism, risking the very engagement-decay failure the problem statement identifies.

7. **Flag 7 — Concept 3 (AI Composed Dialogue)** has zero participant validation and may contradict the universal anti-surveillance instinct (4/4). It should be presented as early-stage hypothesis.

## 9. Recommendation for Committee Presentation

**Lead with strength.** The effort barrier (Cluster A) and low-content connection (Cluster D) are your bedrock — 4/4, independently confirmed, unprompted. Build outward from there.

**Present the three concepts at their actual evidence levels.** The Paired Calendar is your most developed concept with the strongest data connection. The Clock Experiments are a philosophical exploration. The AI Composed Dialogue is an open question. A committee will respect you more for saying "this one is speculative and here's why" than for presenting three equally polished concepts that don't hold up under questioning.

**Own the philosophical moves.** "Make the gap the material" is your distinctive contribution. It is what makes this thesis worth reading. But the strongest version of that argument acknowledges: "My participants experience the gap as a constraint. I am proposing that it can be reframed as material. That reframe is my design argument, and here is what I intend to test." That is intellectually honest and positions you as a researcher who knows the difference between evidence and interpretation.

**The unresolved tension is a feature.** The opacity-vs-transparency tension (P1/P4 want to hide; P2 wants to read) is genuinely unresolved. Don't resolve it artificially. The Paired Calendar's ambiguity — same gesture, different readings — is the resolution. Name it.
